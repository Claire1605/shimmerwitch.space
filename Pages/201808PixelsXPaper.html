In 2018 some people from the Bitsy community ran an event called Pixels x Paper at Babycastles in New York City. I wrote a piece for the companion zine to the event, which you can download here.


On Bitsy

Claire Morley

I get pretty excited when I learn to use a new cool creative thing and always want to share it and allow other people to experience that excitement too. It’s amazing to see the different ways that they might use it, and what they might gain from it. One of the first things I wanted to do when I started using Bitsy was to tell everyone else how amazing Bitsy is.

Bitsy taught me some really important lessons about game design, particularly about scope and limitations – making something manageable and complete, whatever that meant to each project (maybe that means it’s not complete at all). These were lessons that I’d heard before but never really put into practice. Bitsy enforces it through its design, and at the same time allows you to make something that feels cohesive and hand-crafted, complete but with boundaries stretching beyond itself.

I loved that it was so easy to learn and to use, but at the time there wasn’t much documentation or anything in the way of a tutorial on how to use Bitsy, so I made one and put it online a few weeks after I made my first Bitsy game. Talking to people online about it after was so lovely, and was the first time I’d really got involved in an online community for anything. The Bitsy community is one of the best things about Bitsy – people are very friendly, supportive and respectful, and I think a lot of that comes down to (obviously the people being great, but also) the fact that it’s built around a tool that’s so creative and accessible. I wanted to spread the culture of Bitsy – Bitsy culture is sharing and creativity.

I’ve helped run 5 Bitsy workshops so far, all at quite different events to different audiences: game design students at a university; children and their parents at a tech fair; one lunchtime in my office of developers, HR staff and business analysts; A MAZE festival in Berlin; a local women’s game developer meetup. At all of the workshops people pick up Bitsy within minutes, and most have a playable game within an hour. Bitsy gives people an opportunity to express themselves in a medium they might not have experienced creating in before, and I love being able to help open that up to as many people as possible. But, the great thing about Bitsy is that it’s not just a tool for people new to game development, it’s a tool for everyone and has so much value and potential. For me, it allowed me to focus on the core components of a game and its narrative and to filter out a lot of the noise I normally feel when designing games. The limitations allowed me to focus, create, and then move on to a new area of the design, while keeping the central theme in sight.

My pace of making Bitsy games has slowed down in the last few months, and sometimes I feel like I *should* be making more, but I think that Bitsy has opened me up to thinking about game design in lots of new ways, and has given me lots to think about when I’m developing with other tools too. Like the games its developers make, Bitsy’s boundaries stretch far beyond itself.
